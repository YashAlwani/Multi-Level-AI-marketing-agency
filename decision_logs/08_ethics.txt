DL-08 — Ethics: what this system does to a shrinking profession
Marketing Agent POC
(Required by Project_case.md: "Your decision log must grapple with this reality.")


0. Context

This pipeline automates copywriting, audience analysis, and campaign strategy.
The data on what's happening to these roles is not a projection — it's documented
in the assignment brief and visible in job posting trends. This DL is the honest
account.


1. Research question

What ethical responsibility exists when building a system that automates the
work of a profession in measurable decline — and what accountability gaps
does the system leave open?

3. What makes a good decision here

- The facts are stated without softening them
- The counterargument is included — not a one-sided critique
- Accountability gaps in the current design are named specifically
- What the system is and isn't are both clear


4. What I decided

Build the system, document the limits, and make those limits visible in the
output. The pipeline is a starting point, not a finished campaign. The compliance
check is necessary but not exhaustive. The persona is a hypothesis, not market
research.


5. Why this decision

Method: Field (the data is from documented industry sources, not inference)

The facts: copywriters down 28% YoY for two consecutive years. One Dutch agency
went from 290 copywriters to 20 after implementing AI. This system automates
the exact same tasks — copy generation, audience analysis, campaign strategy.
It costs $0 to run. The barrier to entry for replacing a freelance copywriter
is an OpenRouter account and a laptop.

The counterargument is real: creative leadership roles are growing. "AI doesn't
replace creativity — it replaces execution." The senior strategist managing AI
systems is a growing role. What's being compressed is the execution layer —
the junior copywriter writing the third iteration of someone else's brief.

The problem with the counterargument: entry-level execution is where most people
start. The path from junior copywriter to senior creative director goes through
exactly the work this pipeline now does in 90 seconds. Removing that on-ramp
narrows the pipeline of future senior talent. That effect isn't captured in current
job posting data.

Three accountability gaps in this specific design:

  1. No inter-agent critique. vision.py can hallucinate a product tag
     ("organic" for a non-organic product) → audience.py builds a persona on it
     → copywriter.py writes "certified organic" copy → guardrails.py doesn't
     catch it (no pattern for organic claims) → it goes out. No agent reviews
     another agent's work. Human review is the only check.

  2. Compliance coverage is not exhaustive. Seven patterns catch seven known
     categories. Novel phrasing slips through. The check is necessary, not
     sufficient.

  3. Persona bias. nomic-embed-text and mistral:7b reflect training data
     distributions. "Eco-conscious millennial" from a water bottle image is
     what the training data most associates with those visual signals — it is not
     market research. A different demographic might be equally viable. The system
     defaults without surfacing that it's defaulting.

What I'd recommend:
Don't describe the output as "ready to publish." Describe it as "a professional
starting point requiring human review." The language matters — if a user believes
the compliance check is exhaustive, they won't look for what it misses.

GAP found:
None of the three accountability gaps above are closed in the current system.
They are documented here so that anyone using the output knows to apply
human judgment before anything goes live.


6. Does this hold up

- Facts stated accurately:       ✅ workforce data cited from Project_case.md
- Counterargument included:      ✅ creative leadership growth is real and noted
- Accountability gaps named:     ✅ three specific gaps with mechanism described
- System limits visible in UI:   🟡 compliance flags are shown; persona bias and
                                     inter-agent error propagation are not visible
                                     to the user at all

Assumptions I'm making:
The user reads the compliance flags rather than ignoring them. If the UI makes
it easy to export without reviewing the flags, the system is less safe than
it appears.

What surprised me:
Writing this DL after the system worked changed how I read the output. A persona
label that looked specific during testing ("Eco-Conscious Millennial Athlete")
looks like a guess once you consider what the model is actually doing —
pattern-matching against training data distributions, not researching a market.


7. What this unlocks

Next LO stage: Managing → ongoing

What I can now do:
Be honest in any presentation of this system about what it does and doesn't
provide. The three gaps above are the answer to "what would you fix in v2?"

Ongoing monitoring:
Watch for moments where the system produces confident-looking output that is
factually wrong (hallucinated product specs, invented statistics). These are
the failure modes that matter most in a real deployment.

How I'll know this worked:
If someone reviewing the portfolio asks "what are the risks?" and the answer
is already documented here — the DL did its job.
