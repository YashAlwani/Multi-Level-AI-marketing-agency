====================================================================
 MARKETING AGENT — THE GAP ANALYSIS
 How a campaign is made today, and how it changes with the agent
====================================================================

WHAT THIS IS
------------
This is the before-and-after story that justifies the whole project.
"Before" is how a marketing campaign gets made inside a human agency.
"After" is how the same work happens once a team of specialist AI agents
does it. Every feature in the rest of the system traces back to a gap
shown here, so this document comes first.

The project brief sets the stakes plainly. Copywriter roles fell 28% in a
year, for two years running. One Dutch agency went from 290 copywriters to
20 after adopting AI. The work most exposed is the execution layer — the
research, the audience definition, the third draft of someone else's brief.
This system automates exactly that layer, so the gap it closes is real and
already being closed in the market.


THE WORK TODAY (AS-IS)
----------------------
A campaign passes from hand to hand. A client sends a brief. An account
planner researches the product and the market, then defines a target
audience by hand. A copywriter drafts variants, rewrites them after
internal feedback, and waits. A legal reviewer reads every line for risky
claims and kicks anything doubtful back to the writer. A conversion
specialist tunes the call to action for the channel. Then it goes to the
client, who asks for revisions, and parts of the loop run again.

Even the smallest agency performs five distinct functions to do this:
product and market analysis, audience definition, copy and creative,
conversion and call-to-action work, and compliance review. No single
person owns the whole chain well — a creative director makes weak media
calls, a media buyer writes weak copy. The phases need genuinely different
skills, which is why agencies split them across roles in the first place.

The cost of that split is time and hand-offs. The job is measured in days,
most of it spent waiting, relaying, and re-reviewing.

THE WORK WITH THE AGENT (TO-BE)
-------------------------------
The same five functions become five places in one automated pass:

- Product and market analysis -> the vision and color agents read the
  product image into tags, signals, mood, and a palette.
- Audience definition -> the audience agent turns those signals into one
  named persona with an age range, interests, and platform behaviour.
- Copy and creative -> the copywriter agent writes two variants for that
  persona, one emotional and one benefit-led.
- Compliance review -> the guardrails check scans every line for risky
  claims with plain rules, in well under a second.
- Conversion -> the cta_optimizer agent scores each call to action and
  suggests a stronger one.

What was days of relay becomes about a minute of machine work. The one
human step the "after" picture keeps on purpose is review: a person reads
the report and refines it, the same judgement an account manager applies
before a client sees the work — and nothing else.

WHY KEEP A HUMAN IN THE LOOP
----------------------------
The brief is blunt about the risk: this kind of system eliminates
middle-class jobs, and one agent's mistake can become another agent's
input. So the design does not pretend the output is finished. The campaign
is presented as a professional starting point that needs human review, the
compliance flags are shown rather than silently fixed, and the refinement
panel exists so a person stays in control of tone, audience, and wording.
The limits are documented and visible, not hidden.


WHY IT'S BUILT THIS WAY
-----------------------
Because the gap is structural, not cosmetic. The agencies split the work
across five roles for a reason — each phase needs different context — so
the agents mirror that same split rather than asking one model to do
everything at once. The decision to use five specialists instead of one
generalist is tested in DL-02; the human-review stance is argued in DL-08.
Every later decision log points back to a gap on this page.
